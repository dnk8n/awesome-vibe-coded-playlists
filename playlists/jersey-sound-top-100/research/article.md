# The Jersey Sound — The FIDA/ThinkSoul Top 100 (30 Years Later)

A faithful, Discogs-verified rebuild of the community's own canon: **"The 'Jersey Sound' — Top
100 Tunes (30 Years Later and the Music Lives On!)"**, compiled by the FIDA/ThinkSoul Committee
and published by Christopher Flowers in December 2019 (Flowers, 2019a; 2019b). The list is
reproduced **exactly as ranked** — this is the scene's reckoning, not a re-curation. Every entry
has been verified against the Discogs API for its exact credited artist, track title, label and
pressing date; YouTube links are drawn from each release page's own community-added videos. The
table below is sorted by **release date** to read as a timeline, but each row keeps its original
**Rank** (1 = the scene's #1). Built with the
[discogs-playlist skill](../../skill/discogs-playlist/). (Curator: Dean Kayton, July 2026.)

**On the two blank slots.** The source list skips from 57 to 60 — slots **58 and 59 were left
empty**, and Flowers explicitly invited readers to nominate "any true 'Jersey Sound' House tunes
that should have made the list" (Flowers, 2019b). Those two seats are filled here with the
curator's picks, flagged **[FILL]** in the table and reasoned in the notes: **#58 Blaze –

original, and **#59 Kerri Chandler – "Hallelujah"** (King Street, 1996), gospel-house from the
one producer Flowers names in the essay yet the list itself omits.

## What the Jersey Sound was

Between roughly **1985 and 1992**, producers in **Newark and Essex County, New Jersey** built a
regional dialect of house music that the scene came to call the **Jersey Sound** — soulful,
melodic and vocal-led, sung in an R&B and gospel vernacular over Chicago-derived drum machines
(Flowers, 2019a). Where Chicago's early house was track-based and "jacking," the Jersey records
foregrounded **chords, songwriting and the singer** — what Flowers calls putting "a bowtie" on
the Chicago sound. In the United Kingdom the same music was received under a different name,
**garage house** (Wikipedia, 2024; Brewster, 2017), and it became one of the most globally
influential strains of soulful house.

The scene's epicentre was **Club Zanzibar** in Newark, open since 1979 and, by the mid-1980s,
a room that mixed Chicago house with Italo-disco, UK jazz-funk, boogie and disco classics
(Flowers, 2019a; Yglesias, 2023). Its architecture and sound system deliberately echoed New
York's Paradise Garage (Yglesias, 2023). The records that would define the Jersey Sound came
out of home studios in Newark neighbourhoods — Flowers names North 6th Street and Sussex
Avenue — where local producers added melody and voice to the imported template.

## Tony Humphries, Movin' Records, and the machine

The style's chief tastemaker was **Tony Humphries**, Zanzibar's resident DJ from 1982 (Yglesias,
2023), who broke local records both on the club's floor and on his weekly **WRKS-FM "98.7 KISS
Mastermix"** radio show (Flowers, 2019a). His supply line was **Movin' Records**, an East Orange
shop-and-label on Central Avenue that armed Humphries for the club and the airwaves. That triangle
— **club, radio show, record shop/label** — is what carried Newark and Essex County producers to
international recognition (Flowers, 2019a).

Four production camps drove the bulk of the output between 1985 and 1992: **Blaze Productions,
Smack Music, Ace Beat/B.O.P and Backroom** (Flowers, 2019a). Around them worked producers
including **James Bratton, Kerri Chandler, Jovonn, Kyle "Small" Smith, Stephon Johnson and
Shedrick Guy** (Flowers, 2019a). Humphries and Zanzibar are today credited, alongside Larry
Levan and the Paradise Garage, as principal architects of garage house (Wikipedia, 2024).

## How Newark reached the world

Club regulars routinely taped Humphries' KISS-FM Mastermix broadcasts before heading out; those
cassettes reached London, and **by 1987 one of the first "Jersey Sound" DJ/artist tours** was
organised by UK promoters (Flowers, 2019a). By **October 1989 the sound broke across the UK**,
prompting a second London trip on which Humphries brought Zanzibar patrons and staff. During the
**1990 New Music Seminar** in New York, a **"Jersey Jams" showcase** — sponsored by Movin'
Records and Club Zanzibar — became an unofficial highlight of the week, after which the **Jersey
Sound Coalition** was formed by Movin' and allied producers to coordinate marketing and artist
development (Flowers, 2019a). The music continued past 1992 through a second wave of local
producers — Kerri Chandler foremost among them (Yglesias, 2023) — and its soulful signature is
still audible in today's soulful house.

## Method and caveats

The **ranking follows the source list**; everything else is anchored to Discogs. The link prefers
the record's **master release**, and the **Artist and Track are taken character-for-character from
that Discogs page**: the Artist as credited (the per-track credit on splits/compilations, else the
master credit — stylised spellings and all), and the Track a single tracklist item — the version
the list named, or, where none was named, an **extended/long** cut, else a **club** cut, else the
first A-side (a radio/edit only as a last resort). When a named mix isn't on the master the row
**stays on the master** with its best cut — users can click **"See all versions"** there to reach
a specific pressing. The source list's own wording only drove the search; extra track detail and a
★ quality rating ride in the Notes/Confidence. Several entries the source listed only as **acetates or
demos** (#23, #49, #64, #87) proved to have catalogued Discogs masters after all and were included
rather than faked. All 100 ranked slots are filled with verified Discogs releases.
YouTube links follow the skill's matching ladder and 15-minute rule; any deviation is marked ▶.
When no mix was named, the pick favours the **club version** (an extended/long club if present),
else the first A-side cut. The Confidence column carries a **★ rating**: ★★★★★ means the ideal cut
(a club/extended or the prompt's named mix) paired with an exact video; anything lower flags a slot
worth revisiting for a better cut or upload (e.g. releases that only offered a radio edit).
