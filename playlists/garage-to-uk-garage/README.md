# From Paradise Garage to UK Garage — The Evolution

**26 records, ~20 years, one unbroken lineage.** This playlist traces how a New York
disco club's sound became a New Jersey scene, crossed the Atlantic as an import, mutated
under jungle's bass pressure, and came back out the other side as *UK garage*. Play it in
order and the genealogy plays itself: 1980 soul-disco at one end, skippy 2-step at the other,
and no single seam where "garage" stops being one thing and starts being another.

## The thesis

The word **"garage" names a place, not a tempo.** It comes from the [**Paradise Garage**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradise_Garage),
the New York club where **Larry Levan** was resident DJ from 1977 until it closed on 1 October 1987.
Levan's sound — gospel-drenched, dub-wise, disco built for a congregation — was so identified with the
room that when British record shops needed a name for the soulful American house they were importing a
decade later, they reached for the club: *garage*. So the genre that the UK eventually made its own is
named after a Manhattan address, by way of Newark, New Jersey.

This set is the connective tissue told as a night out. It moves through **five movements** — and every
row is anchored to a real Discogs release page, with the audio drawn from that page's own community-attached
videos, so nothing here is a vibe I've invented. The table is the chronological spine; the story below is
how the records talk to each other.

### I. Paradise Garage — the foundation (1980–1983)

Before "house," before "garage" was a genre, there was the room and the records Levan played in it.
**Loleatta Holloway's "Love Sensation"** is the voice that half of dance music would go on to sample;
**Taana Gardner's "Heartbeat"** is the impossibly slow, impossibly heavy groove that only made sense at
Levan's tempo; and **NYC Peech Boys' "Don't Make Me Wait"** — Levan's own group — is the missing link
made audible: dub-disco with cavernous space where a lesser producer would have put more notes. That
echo, that patience, is the DNA everything downstream inherits. **First Choice** and **Class Action**
(the latter mixed end-to-end by Levan himself) close the movement as pure Garage anthems.

### II. Club Zanzibar & the Jersey Sound — the sound gets a home (1985–1989)

When the Garage shut, the flame moved across the Hudson. At [**Club Zanzibar**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Club_Zanzibar)
in Newark — built, literally, around a copy of the Paradise Garage's sound system — **Tony Humphries**
had been resident since 1982, and his 98.7 KISS-FM mastermixes broadcast the emerging *Jersey sound* to
the whole tri-state area. This is where garage becomes electronic: **Colonel Abrams' "Trapped"** was an
anthem in *both* clubs, straddling the disco-to-house cut; **Cultural Vibe's "Ma Foom Bey"** is Zanzibar
in one record; and **Blaze** (the Newark trio) and **Adeva** turn the gospel-house diva sound into a
template. It's soulful, vocal, church-schooled — and it is unmistakably the same lineage, now programmed
instead of played by a band.

### III. The bridge — what the UK actually imported (1992–1996)

British DJs bought these American records by the crate. Three hinges matter most. **Robin S's "Show Me
Love"** (the StoneBridge mix) hands the UK its signature organ-bass stab. **MK's chopped, sub-heavy dub
of Nightcrawlers' "Push The Feeling On"** is the single most-copied sound-design idea in what came next.
And crucially, one of the era's key producers was himself a New Jersey man: **Todd Edwards** (from
Bloomfield), whose cut-up "sample garage" — vocals diced into a melodic-percussive honeycomb — was, in the
words of the scene, *a lit match to the UK's fuse*. **Kerri Chandler**, son of a Jersey club DJ, keeps the
deep-garage thread alive across the decade. The Atlantic crossing is happening in these five records.

### IV. Speed Garage — the mutation (1996–1997)

Then jungle happened to it. UK DJs sped the imports up, and producers steeped in drum & bass welded
reggae-weight basslines to garage's swing. **Armand Van Helden's "Dark Garage" remix of Sneaker Pimps'
"Spin Spin Sugar"** is widely credited with *starting speed garage* — dragging jungle and D&B ravers onto
the garage floor. **Double 99's "Ripgroove," 187 Lockdown's "Gunman,"** and, above all, **Kelly G's
Bump-N-Go remix of Tina Moore's "Never Gonna Let You Go"** — a 1995 US R&B single re-tooled in 1997 into a
UK Top 10 hit — laid the blueprint. The sound is now recognisably British, but its bones are still Jersey.

### V. 2-Step & UK Garage — the destination (1997–2000)

The bassline calms; the drums start to skip and swing off the four-four. **Groove Chronicles** and
**Dem 2** build the dark, sophisticated underground; **MJ Cole's "Sincere"** proves the music can be as
lush as anything on a major; **Wookie's "Battle"** and **Sweet Female Attitude's "Flowers"** (the Sunship
mix) are soulful 2-step at its peak; and **Artful Dodger & Craig David's "Re-Rewind"** carries the whole
twenty-year lineage into the Christmas 1999 Top 5. **Zed Bias** and **Shanks & Bigfoot** round out a sound
that is, finally, *UK garage* — and still, if you've been listening from the top, obviously the
great-grandchild of a record Larry Levan played in 1982.

## Method & provenance

- **Source of truth:** each pick is anchored to a **Discogs master release**; Artist and Track are taken
  verbatim from that page's tracklist. Where a Discogs credit differs from how a record is usually billed,
  the page wins and the difference is noted (e.g. *Peech Boys* vs. *NYC Peech Boys*; *Class Action Featuring
  Chris Wiltshire*; *M.J. Cole Feat. Caspar Nova & Jay Dee*).
- **Audio:** YouTube links come from each release page's own embedded videos, chosen by a match ladder
  (exact mix → base title → alternate) and then **validated against the YouTube Data API — 26/26 public and
  playable.** A few cuts are deliberately hand-pinned (▶ notes in the table): the Kelly G remix of Tina
  Moore, MK's Nocturnal Dub, the full Ripgroove, the Sunship mix of "Flowers."
- **Saved YouTube playlist:** the ordered, machine-readable `playlist_items.json` and an idempotent
  `create_playlist.py` are included. A durable saved playlist wasn't minted in this session because the
  YouTube Data API project's **daily quota was exhausted** (playlist writes cost 50 units each); re-running
  `python3 create_playlist.py --token <ya29…>` against a project with fresh quota creates it in ~1 minute.
  The instant-listen link at the top needs none of that.
