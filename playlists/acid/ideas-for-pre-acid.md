Finding an exact match for the Roland TB-303 (released in 1982) prior to 1980 is tricky, as the 303's iconic "squelch" stems from its highly unique 4-pole diode ladder filter layout and its accent/slide sequencer. However, if you are looking for monophonic analog synthesizers built up until 1980 that could produce similar rubbery, resonant, or liquid basslines, look into these specific models: [1, 2, 3, 4] 
## Roland SH-09 (1980) and SH-1/SH-2 (1978/1979)
Roland's own late-70s monosynths share a highly compatible genetic DNA with what became the TB-303.

* 
* The Sound: They utilize similar vintage Roland parts and cascading OTA filter designs. When you crank the resonance up on an SH-09 or SH-2, it bubbles and chirps with a very distinct "Roland flavor" that predates the 303.
* The Vibe: Excellent for raw, punchy, rubbery bass, though they lack the specific built-in slide/accent sequencer behavior. [5] 
* 

## Roland System-100 / 100M (1975–1979)
Before making portable monosynths, Roland made semi-modular and modular systems. [5, 6] 

* 
* The Sound: The Model 101 keyboard component from the System-100 has an incredibly squelchy 4-pole low-pass filter.
* The Vibe: By driving the inputs and modulating the envelope decays sharply, it achieves a liquid, driving bass tone highly reminiscent of a raw 303. [7, 8, 9] 
* 

## Korg MS-10 and MS-20 (1978)
While the TB-303 filter is famous for a smooth but unstable diode design, the early Korg MS-series filter is famous for being incredibly aggressive.

* 
* The Sound: The early Korg IC-35 filter circuit can be driven into extreme self-oscillation.
* The Vibe: If you back the peak off just right and use a snappy pitch envelope, it mimics the wet, screaming, distorted "acid" tone that people usually achieve by running a 303 through a distortion pedal. [5] 
* 

## EMS Synthi A / VCS3 (1969)
Though vastly different in layout, this legendary British avant-garde instrument is a secret weapon for 303-like textures.

* 
* The Sound: The Synthi utilizes a diode ladder filter—the exact same core topology used in the TB-303 filter layout.
* The Vibe: Because it relies on diodes rather than a traditional Moog transistor ladder, the resonance behaves in an unstable, squelchy, and highly liquid fashion when modulated rapidly. [10, 11] 
* 

## Micromoog (1975)
Most Moog synthesizers have a distinct, creamy 24dB "fat" sound quite different from the 303. However, the Micromoog is an exception.

* 
* The Sound: It features an audio-rate modulation section and an open, aggressive filter sub-circuit.
* The Vibe: It can create chirping, tearing, and rubber-band style basslines that cut through a mix in a very similar percussive fashion. [5] 
*

The closest historical equivalents to that 303 pulse in Moroder’s pre-1982 arsenal include:
## 1. Moog Modular System 3P (with a 16-Step Sequencer)
This was Moroder’s ultimate weapon for tracks like Donna Summer’s "I Feel Love" (1977) and his solo album From Here to Eternity (1977). [2, 3] 

* 
* Why it sounded close: The 303 is famous for its relentless 16-step patterns. Moroder used a Moog analog sequencer to drive a continuous, robotic 16th-note bass loop. [3, 4] 
* The Squelch Factor: While Moog filters are typically warmer and "fatter" than the sharp TB-303, Moroder and his engineer, Robbie Wedel, cranked the filter envelope decay to be incredibly tight and snappy. This gave the Moog a percussive, rubbery pluck that shares a direct sonic lineage with the 303. [5, 6] 
* The Secret Trick: To get that signature rolling "gallop," they didn't program every note; they fed a simple 8th-note baseline into a tempo-locked tape delay, creating a wet, echoing 16th-note pattern. [4, 6] 
* 

## 2. Minimoog Model D (1970)
For his touring rigs and faster studio tracks—including the iconic synth-rock pulse of "Chase" (Midnight Express OST, 1978)—Moroder relied heavily on the Minimoog. [7, 8, 9] 

* 
* Why it sounded close: By using a single sawtooth wave, dropping the sustain to zero, and setting a sharp, biting decay on the filter envelope, the Minimoog can emulate a very aggressive, punchy bassline.
* The Difference: It lacked the automated "slide/glide" circuit built into the 303 sequencer, meaning Moroder's basslines felt more rigidly mechanical and driving, rather than "fluid" or "acidic."
* 

## 3. Roland System-100 / 100M (Late 1970s)
Though Moroder is synonymous with Moog, Munich's Musicland Studios (which Moroder founded) integrated early Roland modular gear as it became available in the late 70s. [1, 10, 11] 

* 
* Why it sounded close: As mentioned previously, Roland’s 1970s filter designs carried the exact chirping, resonant characteristics that eventually evolved into the TB-303. Driving a Roland System module via an external CV/Gate sequencer yielded the closest possible tonal relative to a 303 before the 1980s.
*
